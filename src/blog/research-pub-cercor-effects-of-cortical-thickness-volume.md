<h2>New paper in <i>Cerebral Cortex</i></h2>

I'm excited to share that my first first-author paper out of the [Functional Neuroimaging of Memory lab](https://fnim.utdallas.edu/)—<i>Effects of cortical thickness, volume, and memory performance on age differences in neural reinstatement of scene information</i>—is now [published in <i>Cerebral Cortex</i>](https://doi.org/10.1093/cercor/bhaf213)! 

In this study, we examined how structural brain measures (specifically whole-brain cortical thickness and volume) relate to age differences in <strong>neural reinstatement</strong> of scene information in brain regions that process scene information like the parahippocampal place area and the medial place area.


  <div class="blockquote">
    <strong>Neural reinstatement</strong> is the reactivation of brain activity patterns that occurred during encoding (memory formation) at retrieval (memory recall).
  </div>

  For a quick summary and visuals from the paper, check out my [original post on Bluesky](https://bsky.app/profile/josholivier.bsky.social/post/3lxaoerzakc2n).

<h2>Our main findings</h2>

We found that whole-brain cortical volume predicted reinstatement strength in both parahippocampal and medial place areas. In other words, individuals with greater cortical volume showed stronger reinstatement of scene-related activity.

Strikingly, we also found that cortical volume fully mediated the commonly observed association between age and reinstatement in these regions, meaning differences in reinstatement strength were explained by cortical volume, independently of age.
