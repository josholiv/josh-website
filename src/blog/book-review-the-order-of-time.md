This was my second read of Carlo Rovelli's *The Order of Time*, and it was just as good as the first. Rovelli manages to take a huge and complex topic and break it down into relatively simple terms. I also like that the book is written with a sort of conversational feel, with the discussion ebbing and flowing as if Rovelli were having an informal chat with the reader. Perhaps listening to the audiobook version (which is excellently narrated by Benedict Cumberbatch) added to this "conversational" feel.

On this second read, a couple of things stood out to me. The first is Rovelli's reverence for scientists and philosophers who made early contributions to our collective understanding of time, especially [Anaximander](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaximander). Rovelli considers Anaximander to be the world's first scientist, and the title of the book is derived from his only surviving fragment of original writing:

> Things are transformed, one into another, according to necessity, and render justices to one another, according to the order of time.  
>
> — Anaximander

As with his explanations of physics concepts, Rovelli does a great job of condensing the history of philosophical thought on time into a really accessible narrative.

The second thing that stood out to me is that Rovelli argues that time itself isn't really a "thing" as we tend to understand it. Time exists because the entropy of systems always increases (the second law of thermodynamics), but it is not unified and is not itself a fundamental property of the universe. He argues that we, as humans, understand the flow of time through our memories, which allow us to distinguish between past, present, and future events and have a continuing sense of self. But this understanding comes from the way our brains evolved to form and retrieve memories, and is a limited view of how time actually works.

## Rating

I give this book four out of five stars. This is a book that is so thought-provoking yet so easy to read, and my second read of it was just as enjoyable as the first. I'll likely be back for a third read down the road.
