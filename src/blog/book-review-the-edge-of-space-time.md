I'm so glad that I happened to be in the right time and space to stumble across this book (scrolling through my library's Available Now section on [Libby](https://libbyapp.com/interview/welcome#do-you-have-a-card). Chanda Prescod-Weinstein's The Edge of Space-Time: Particles, Poetry, and the Cosmic Dream Boogie is packed with descriptions of concepts in theoretical physics and how they intertwine with the story of humanity. When I picked up this book, I was expecting something along the lines of Stephen Hawking's *[A Brief History of Time](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/3869.A_Brief_History_of_Time?ac=1&from_search=true&qid=9OzyFlA7hp&rank=2)* or Carlo Rovelli's *[The Order of Time](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/36442813-the-order-of-time?from_search=true&from_srp=true&qid=ISzrcu8LeJ&rank=1)*—two great books on time. The Edge of Space-Time, however, extends the discussion of space-time and cosmology by highlighting perspectives that have historically been excluded from scientific discourse. Prescod-Weinstein's take on the importance of cultivating diversity of thought within the scientific community, and her call to action to recognize that participating in science is an inherently political endeavor, is especially refreshing given the grim backdrop of current global politics.

Even though I'm not exactly the most well-versed person in particle physics or quantum such-and-such, I thoroughly enjoyed Prescod-Weinstein's explanations of scientific principles and theories (though, admittedly, a lot of it flew over my head). I was especially excited to read about a couple of more obscure (to me) physics concepts that I've only ever heard referenced in works of science fiction. Of course, I'm talking about the cosmic microwave background, which is used as a plot point in Liu Cixin's *[The Three-Body Problem](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/20518872-the-three-body-problem?ref=nav_sb_ss_1_18)* book series. The discussion of quantum indeterminacy and its implications, including whether it leaves space for free will or a deterministic model of the universe, was also enlightening and thought-provoking.

<div class="blockquote">“We are forced to reckon with the fact that science is political.”</div>

Perhaps my biggest takeaway from this book is Prescod-Weinstein's unabashed criticism of contemporary issues in science and ethics, such as devastating cuts to federal research funding in the US, and the growing trend of scientific endeavors being connected to the ultra-wealthy and the military-industrial complex. This book has had me thinking about how our approach to scientific research should, sadly, be very different from what it is in 2026.
